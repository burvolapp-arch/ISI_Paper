\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Principal findings}

This paper has presented the first complete empirical application of
the ISI methodology to the EU-27, producing a composite ranking that
integrates concentration indices across six strategic domains.  The
principal empirical findings can be summarised as follows.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{The EU-27 is moderately concentrated on average.}
    The mean composite ISI is \num{0.33815860} with a standard
    deviation of \num{0.06644399}.  Only one country (Malta) exceeds
    the ``highly concentrated'' threshold of 0.50.

  \item \textbf{The ranking is compressed.}  The interquartile range
    is \num{0.09952662} and the Gini coefficient is \num{0.1155},
    indicating that the bulk of EU countries cluster in a narrow
    band of moderate concentration.  This compression has policy
    implications: small changes in underlying data could shift
    individual ranks by several positions, even though the
    substantive concentration level changes little.

  \item \textbf{Defence and logistics drive the ranking.}
    These two axes jointly account for 69.36\% of composite variance
    (\cref{tab:variance-decomp}).  Defence is the dominant axis for
    16 of 27 countries; logistics for the remaining 11.  No other
    axis ever holds the maximum position.

  \item \textbf{The financial axis is informationally redundant for
    ranking purposes.}  Its variance contribution is 1.03\%, its CV
    is low, and its LOO Spearman correlation with the baseline is
    0.993.  However, it may carry independent policy significance
    (e.g., for financial stability assessment).

  \item \textbf{The ranking is robust to most perturbations.}
    Winsorization preserves the ranking perfectly ($\rho = 1.000$).
    Dirichlet weight perturbation produces a mean $\rho$ of 0.979.
    Only defence-axis removal ($\rho = 0.833$) and z-score
    standardisation ($\rho = 0.896$) produce moderate rank changes.

  \item \textbf{Country profiles are heterogeneous.}  The radar
    charts (\cref{sec:country-profiles}) reveal three archetypal
    patterns: small-island concentration (Malta, Cyprus),
    sector-specific spikes (Ireland on technology, Netherlands on
    defence), and broad diversification (Germany, France).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The small-island hypothesis}

Malta and Cyprus occupy the top two positions.  Both are small island
economies with limited domestic production capacity, narrow port
infrastructure, and concentrated defence procurement channels.  This
raises the question of whether the ISI mechanically penalises small
countries.

The answer is nuanced.  The ISI measures \emph{concentration of
external dependencies}, not economic size per se.  However, small
economies have fewer feasible counterparties and less diversified
infrastructure, which leads to structurally higher concentration
scores.  This is not a measurement artefact---it reflects a genuine
strategic vulnerability.  A small country with a single deep-water
port \emph{is} more logistics-concentrated than a large country with
multiple ports, and this concentration represents a real policy
challenge.

Nevertheless, users of the ISI should interpret the ranking with
awareness of the size confound.  Future versions of the index could
explore size-normalised variants (e.g., conditioning on GDP or
population) as a complementary perspective, though such normalisation
would alter the index's strategic interpretation.

\subsection{Interpretation boundaries}

Several limitations bound the interpretation of the results presented
in this paper:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Single time period.}  The ISI~v1.0 snapshot is
    cross-sectional (2024 reference year).  No inference about trends
    or dynamic adjustment is possible.  A time-series extension is
    planned for future work.

  \item \textbf{Equal weighting.}  The composite assigns
    $w_j = 1/6$ to each axis.  While the Dirichlet perturbation
    exercise demonstrates robustness to moderate weight variation,
    a policy-maker who judges defence concentration to be
    \emph{more important} than financial concentration could
    legitimately prefer a weighted variant.  The ISI methodology
    (\cite{isi_paper1}) explicitly supports user-specified weights.

  \item \textbf{Axis construction choices.}  Each axis relies on
    specific data sources and normalisation procedures.  The
    z-score exercise (\cref{sec:zscore}) demonstrates that
    alternative normalisation can produce moderately different
    rankings ($\rho = 0.896$).

  \item \textbf{Missing granularity.}  The ISI aggregates
    sub-indicators within each axis.  Countries that score
    identically on the composite may have very different
    sub-indicator profiles.  The axis-level radar charts provide
    partial disaggregation, but sub-indicator-level analysis is
    beyond the scope of this paper.

  \item \textbf{No causal claims.}  The ISI is a descriptive
    measurement instrument.  High ISI scores indicate concentrated
    dependencies; they do not, by themselves, predict adverse
    outcomes such as supply disruptions or coercive leverage.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Comparison with related indices}

The ISI occupies a distinct position relative to existing
vulnerability and resilience indices:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item The \textbf{EU Critical Raw Materials} assessment
    (\cite{ec_critical_raw}) focuses on material-level supply risk
    and economic importance.  The ISI subsumes a related concept
    within its critical-inputs axis but extends coverage to five
    additional domains.

  \item The \textbf{OECD Composite Indicators Handbook}
    (\cite{oecd_handbook_2008}) provides general methodological
    guidance.  The ISI follows its recommendations on normalisation,
    weighting transparency, and robustness testing.

  \item Trade-concentration indices such as the Herfindahl--Hirschman
    Index (\cite{herfindahl1950,hirschman1964}) underpin individual
    ISI axes but do not aggregate across domains.  The ISI's
    contribution is precisely this multi-domain aggregation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Policy implications}

The results suggest several actionable policy directions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
  \item \textbf{Prioritise defence and logistics diversification.}
    Because these two axes dominate composite variance and axis
    dominance counts, policies aimed at reducing strategic
    dependence should focus on diversifying defence procurement
    partnerships and logistics infrastructure.

  \item \textbf{Monitor rank-volatile countries.}  Countries with
    high Dirichlet rank volatility (Netherlands, Finland, Estonia)
    are positioned near tipping points where modest changes in
    underlying concentration could shift their ranking substantially.
    These countries merit closer monitoring.

  \item \textbf{Distinguish between axes for tailored policy.}
    The near-zero inter-axis correlations between the financial
    axis and all others suggest that financial-dependence reduction
    policies operate independently of trade or infrastructure
    policies.  A country can diversify its financial exposures
    without affecting its logistics or defence concentration, and
    vice versa.

  \item \textbf{Small-state awareness.}  Malta and Cyprus face
    structural concentration that cannot be fully mitigated through
    policy alone.  For these countries, the ISI is best read as a
    risk identifier rather than a performance benchmark.
\end{enumerate}
