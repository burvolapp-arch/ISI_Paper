\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{Scope}\label{sec:intro:scope}

The International Sovereignty Index (\ISI) quantifies the structural
concentration of a country's critical external dependencies.  It does not
measure sovereignty in the normative or legal sense.  It measures the extent to
which a state relies on a small number of foreign partners for essential goods,
services, and capabilities across six domains: financial market access, energy
supply, technology and semiconductor inputs, defence-industrial procurement,
critical raw materials, and logistics and freight infrastructure.

The index operates at the country--year level.  Each observation produces a
composite score on the unit interval \([0,1]\) and a categorical
classification.  Higher values denote greater concentration and, by
implication, greater structural exposure to partner-specific disruption.

\ISI v\,1.0 is materialised for the EU-27.  The architecture is international
by design and extensible to global coverage pending data harmonisation and
integrity validation across additional statistical sources.

\subsection{Context}\label{sec:intro:context}

Existing composite indicators in the sovereignty-adjacent space---the
\textcite{kof2023globalisation} Globalisation Index, the
\textcite{unctad2023productive} Productive Capacities Index, and the
EU~Global Gateway scorecards---measure either \emph{openness} or
\emph{capacity}.  None directly measures \emph{concentration} of external
dependence.  The analytical gap is consequential: a highly open economy with
diversified partners faces a qualitatively different risk profile from one
whose openness is channelled through a single counterparty.

The \ISI addresses this gap.  It is not a substitute for openness or capacity
indicators; it is a complement that captures the structural geometry of
dependence.

\subsection{Design Principles}\label{sec:intro:principles}

Five principles constrain the index architecture:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{P\arabic*}.]
  \item \textbf{Transparency.}  Every formula, data source, and threshold is
    published.  No proprietary model or hidden weighting layer exists.
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility.}  Given identical input data, any competent
    third party must be able to reproduce the published scores to full
    numerical precision.
  \item \textbf{Auditability.}  Cryptographic integrity records (hash chains
    and digital signatures) accompany every published snapshot, enabling
    verification without access to the original computation environment.
  \item \textbf{Parsimony.}  The index uses exactly one concentration
    measure---the Herfindahl--Hirschman Index---applied uniformly across all
    axes.  No axis receives preferential weighting in the composite.
  \item \textbf{Conservatism.}  Where methodological choices are ambiguous,
    the index defaults to the option that is simpler, more established, and
    easier to audit.
\end{enumerate}
